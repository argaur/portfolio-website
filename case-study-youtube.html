YouTube 2.0 — Fixing Long-Form Discovery

Section 1

Problem Context & Background

1.1 The Central Problem

With the rise of short-form video content on YouTube, engagement with long-form videos (10+ minutes) has significantly declined. Shrinking viewer attention spans and the popularisation of short-form content by competing platforms have made it more palatable — leaving traditional long-form content underutilised.

The core thesis: Long-form content is not in structural decline. It still accounts for 70%+ of total YouTube watch time. The problem is feed displacement — Shorts are absorbing mobile browse real estate that used to surface long-form videos.

1.2 Why This Matters for YouTube's Business

YouTube is not just a platform to watch videos. It is a two-sided marketplace where creators, viewers, and advertisers are connected by a matchmaking engine. Any gap in that matchmaking is a gap in value for all three sides.

  • Top creators (>5M subscribers) historically drove maximum engagement through long-form content. Post-Shorts, these creators have seen a measurable decline in long-form views.
  • Declining creator motivation restricts supply of good long-form content. Less supply means the discovery problem compounds over time.
  • YouTube's revenue primarily comes from ads. Long-form earns $3–$6 RPM (per 1,000 views) versus $0.03–$0.07 for Shorts — a 100x gap in per-view monetisation.

1.3 Scope

In Scope

  • Market: India — YouTube's largest and fastest-growing user base
  • Platform: YouTube main app (excluding Music and Kids)
  • Content Type: Long-form videos (10+ minutes)
  • Device: Mobile-first users
  • Age Group: 18–34

Deliberately Out of Scope

  • Ad monetisation structure changes (separate workstream)
  • TV and desktop experience improvements
  • AI summary features and creator revenue decisions
  • YouTube Music, Kids, or Shorts algorithm changes
Section 2

Platform Ecosystem Understanding

2.1 YouTube as a Two-Sided Market

YouTube operates like a farmers market. Creators bring content (supply), viewers come looking for content worth their time (demand), and the platform connects both while monetising through ads placed where the most attention gathers. If sellers stop bringing quality products, buyers leave. If buyers leave, sellers stop creating. Both sides must remain healthy simultaneously.

Right now the market is getting unbalanced. Long-form creators are producing fewer videos. The home feed is not surfacing what viewers came for. Ads are still running — but they are reaching fewer attentive people.

2.2 How the Recommendation System Works

YouTube's recommendation engine has evolved through three major phases:

2005–2011

Optimised for: Clicks. More clicks meant more recommendations. Created a clickbait epidemic — users felt tricked.

2012–2023

Optimised for: Watch time. Clicks alone did not mean someone enjoyed the video. Views dropped 20% when they made the shift — they kept it anyway.

2025 Onward

Optimised for: Satisfaction-weighted discovery. Not just “did they watch?” but “did they feel the time was worth it?”

2.3 The Five Discovery Surfaces

YouTube does not have one feed — it has five distinct surfaces, each doing a different job:

Home Page

Personalised mix of subscriptions, new creators, and trending. Goal: interest expansion and discovery.

Suggested / Up Next

Based on current video and broader watch history. Goal: keep the session going.

Search

User-declared intent — the most direct signal available. Goal: give exactly what was asked for.

Shorts Feed

Swipe-based, passive, mobile-first. Goal: new audience discovery.

Subscriptions

Content from channels the user follows. Goal: loyalty reinforcement.

The critical design gap: Since mid-2025, the Shorts and long-form recommendation systems are fully decoupled — different engines, different signals, different goals. A user's interest expressed through Shorts is invisible to the long-form system. This is the core technical problem our solutions must address.

2.4 Three Structural Blind Spots

  • Mood vs. History: The system knows what you watched last week. It does not know what kind of mood you are in right now. A finance viewer on a lazy Sunday afternoon will still be served finance content.
  • The Mid-Length Gap: Videos between 4 and 8 minutes fall into no man's land — too long to be optimised like Shorts, too short to benefit from long-form algorithm signals.
  • Cold Start for New Creators: A new creator has almost no chance of getting their long video recommended without subscriber data. Shorts have a much easier path to early views — another reason creators prefer them.
Section 3

Primary Research — Survey of 171 Users

3.1 Research Design

This primary research is based on a quantitative and qualitative survey distributed to a pool of 250–300 individuals, capturing 171 responses. The survey was designed to understand user behaviour, intent, and friction points specifically related to long-form video (10+ minutes) consumption on YouTube.

Demographics

  • 25–34 years: ~54%
  • 35–44 years: ~25%
  • 18–24 years: ~12%
  • 45+ years: ~8.5%
  • Gender split: ~55% male, ~45% female

Research Themes

  • General usage and platform intensity
  • Long-form engagement motivation
  • Drop-off triggers and abandonment causes
  • Discovery entry points and search behaviour
  • Feature validation for navigation improvements

3.2 What Viewers Said

The clearest message from viewers: they want long videos, but YouTube is not helping them find or finish them.

59% stop watching because the video feels slow or stretched — not because they lost interest in the topic
47% have to manually search for long videos every time they open the app
32% ever discover a long video just by opening the app without searching
58% do not finish the long videos they start — even when they originally sat down to watch

People are not giving up on long-form content. They are giving up on YouTube’s ability to show it to them. The interest exists. Discovery and retention are failing.

3.3 What the Product Signals Showed

  • 49% of users have to search manually for long videos. The most visited page on YouTube (Home) is actively working against long-form discovery.
  • 22% stop watching a long video because a suggested video on the side caught their eye. The platform is competing against itself.
  • After watching a Short, only 15% actively look for a longer video from the same creator. But 41% say they would watch one if it were easy to find. The demand is there — the pathway is not.

3.4 What Creators Said

  • Making a 10–15 minute video takes 12–20 hours of work. Making five Shorts takes 2–4 hours. The effort gap is enormous, and AI tools have mostly helped Shorts, not long-form.
  • 11% of survey respondents — regular viewers, not creators — said they used to make videos and quit in the last year. This quiet attrition is happening below the surface.

3.5 What Advertisers Are Experiencing

  • 30% of written comments mentioned ads as a frustration — without a single survey question asking about ads. That is how strongly people feel about it.
  • 70% of people watch long videos to learn deeply. Unskippable ads break that concentration at exactly the moment people are most engaged.

Note: The ad problem is real and urgent, but it sits in a different workstream from discovery. We flag it here because it directly affects completion rates, which affect creator revenue, which affects how much long-form content gets made. Everything is connected.

Section 4

Secondary Research & Market Evidence

4.1 Market Reality: Long-Form Is Still the Majority

2.53B Global Monthly Active Users (Jan 2025)
491M India MAU (~20% of global base)
70%+ of total YouTube watch time is long-form
200B Shorts daily views (Jan 2026)

The issue is not lack of long-form consumption — it is feed displacement. Shorts are absorbing mobile browse real estate that used to surface long-form content.

4.2 Monetisation & Creator Incentives

Long-Form

  • Creator RPM: $3–$6 per 1,000 views
  • US CPM (advertiser-side): $15–$33
  • Creator revenue share: 55% of ad revenue

Shorts

  • Creator RPM: $0.03–$0.07 per 1,000 views
  • US CPM (advertiser-side): ~$4
  • Creator revenue share: 45% of shared pool

Creators observe per-view economics, not per-hour parity. This creates a clear tension: Shorts grow faster, long-form pays significantly better per view. Discovery must protect long-form supply incentives.

4.3 Discovery Friction: Behavioural Evidence

  • Average search time per session has risen to 12 minutes — up 60% since 2019. In a typical 40-minute session, that is ~30% of unmonetised time.
  • 19% of all users abandon a YouTube session without watching a single video. Among 18–24 year olds, that figure rises to 29%.
  • 51% of Gen Z call long-form a favourite format. 53.1% actively watch it. The barrier is discovery friction, not format preference.

4.4 The Long-Form vs. Short-Form Picture

  • Long-form remains 70%+ of total watch time and is not collapsing.
  • Short-form engagement is showing fatigue: Shorts interactions fell 47% YoY in 2025 despite post volume surging 71%.
  • Long-form performs strongest on CTV (lean-back behaviour). Shorts dominate mobile discovery surfaces. In mobile-first India, long-form competes directly with swipe behaviour.
Section 5

Competitor Landscape

5.1 How Attention Is Being Fragmented

TikTok

Captures casual, idle scrolling via an endless short-form feed. Conditions users toward low time-commitment viewing before a long session can begin.

Netflix

Captures intentional evening sessions through lean-back, premium long-form content. Taking dedicated, high-intent long-session time away from YouTube.

AI Tools

Capture informational intent through instant responses. Reduces the need to watch a 20-minute explainer video for an answer.

The risk is not user migration to a single competitor — it is the loss of specific viewing moments across multiple platforms. YouTube sits between all of these use cases and must defend each.

5.2 What Other Platforms Tried — and What Failed

Instagram IGTV

Separate long-form tab. Killed March 2022. <7M downloads against 1B+ users. Lesson: Users do not adopt new in-app destinations for long-form.

Netflix “Play Something”

Random content button. Killed Jan 2023. Lesson: Randomness without context reduces trust. Users need to understand why something is recommended.

Netflix Autoplay

Auto-play next episode. Still active. Increased long-form consumption. Lesson: Reducing friction within format works. The win is inside the existing feed, not on new surfaces.

TikTok Long-Form

Extended video duration to 60 minutes. Creators >1 min gaining 5x more followers. Lesson: Even short-form platforms pursue long-form. YouTube’s window to consolidate its advantage is narrowing.

The competitor graveyard is unambiguous: every bolted-on cross-format discovery feature has failed within 2 years. Any solution must be native to the existing feed — not a new surface. This shaped every solution in this PRD.

Section 6

Prioritisation & Pain Points

6.1 Three Core Problems

After completing research, three pain points emerged as highest priority — not three separate problems, but three parts of the same compounding loop.

P1 — “I Can’t Find What I Came For.”

Users open YouTube on mobile but do not naturally see relevant long videos. Most have to manually search every session. When finding content starts to feel like work, users either settle for Shorts or leave entirely.

P2 — “What If This Video Wastes My Time?”

A 20-minute video feels like a commitment. Users cannot easily judge if it will deliver value. Thumbnails look similar. Intros are often slow. Compared to a 30-second Short, starting a long video feels risky.

P3 — “Why Spend 15 Hours on Something That Might Not Get Seen?”

Creators see unpredictable algorithmic visibility for long-form. Shorts provide faster reach and feedback. Over time, creators shift effort toward Shorts — reducing long-form supply, which makes discovery even harder.

6.2 How These Problems Connect

The home feed stops showing long videos → users don’t find them → users who do find them leave early because content is padded → creators see low completion rates and poor earnings → they stop making long videos → the home feed has even less good content to show. It is a loop. The four solutions in this PRD are designed to break it at three different points simultaneously.

What We Are Fixing

  • P1: Deep Dive Tab + Shorts-to-Long Bridge + Viewing Streak
  • P2: Creator Success Suite — quality score before publishing
  • P3: Creator Success Suite — AI tools to reduce production effort

What We Are Deferring

  • Ad frequency / unskippable ads (monetisation decision, separate workstream)
  • TV and desktop experience (must fix mobile discovery first)
  • AI summary features (creator revenue implications out of scope)
Section 7

Primary Persona

The Intentional Learner

We prioritised this persona because the research data directly supports it:

  • 70% of long-form viewers watch to understand something deeply.
  • 47% must manually search for content each session — the algorithm is failing them at the most basic level.
  • 29% of Gen Z sessions end without watching anything. They came, they searched, they left.
  • 41% say they would move from Shorts to long-form if the path were easy. The demand is present. The friction is the barrier.

Who They Are

Age 18–34, mobile-first, purpose-driven. Watching to learn, build a skill, or understand a topic in depth. Not passive browsing.

Their Core Frustration

Time wasted finding the right video. They open the app knowing what they want to learn but cannot trust the home screen to surface it. Search is the fallback, not the exception.

Their Risk

Switches to an AI tool or exits the app entirely. The session is lost — and so is the ad impression, the creator view, and the engagement signal that would have improved future recommendations.

Section 8

Solution 1 — Deep Dive Tab

Vertical Solution: directly addresses P1 — The Home Feed Has Stopped Working for Long-Form

What It Is

A dedicated tab on YouTube positioned second in the navigation bar after Home, built entirely around intentional long-form consumption. Users declare their interest categories — PM, Podcasts, Finance, Football — and the tab organises all long-form content around those declared interests. No Shorts appear anywhere inside it. It is not a feed replacement — it is a purposeful destination a user enters when they want to go deep.

How It Works

On first entry, a 30-second onboarding screen pre-populates 5–6 interest categories from watch history as large visual tiles. The user confirms or adjusts, subscribed creators are automatically mapped into each category, and the tab is live immediately.

Inside each category, four sections stack in priority order:

Continue Watching

Pinned strip at the top for videos started but not finished, each with a progress bar and resume timestamp.

New from Your Creators

Latest videos from subscribed creators in this category, sorted chronologically or by recommendation (user picks during setup).

Deep Picks

Quality-ranked recommendations from creators the user does not yet follow. Each card shows chapter previews and a trust signal like “81% of viewers finish this.”

Creator Spotlight

Rotating weekly to introduce one unfamiliar creator’s top three long-form videos in the category.

Key Innovation: Deep Picks ranks content by completion rate and rewatch signals — not view count. High view count is a popularity signal. Completion rate is a quality signal. For long-form content, these two frequently conflict — and every existing YouTube surface optimises for the wrong one.

Section 9

Solution 2 — Long-Form Viewing Streak

Lateral Solution: addresses P1 through behaviour change, not product surface

What It Is

A weekly topic-based habit mechanic that rewards users for consistently engaging with long-form content in their declared Deep Dive categories. The core unit is a weekly streak — watch 3 qualifying long-form videos (15 min+, 75% completion) in one topic category per week and the streak advances. It lives inside the Deep Dive tab and is designed to feel like a personal viewing progress tracker, not a platform engagement counter.

Qualification Rules

  • Video must be 15 minutes minimum.
  • User must reach 75% completion at any playback speed, including 2x.
  • Chapter-jumping counts if total content covered reaches 75%.
  • One grace week per calendar month protects the streak from a single missed week.

Milestone Progression

The recommendation type itself evolves with the user’s demonstrated commitment:

Week 3 — Consistent Viewer

Badge unlock + 1 curated long-form video recommendation in the declared category. Makes the streak tangible.

Week 6 — Personalised Summary

Hours watched, topics covered. Recommendation type shifts from individual videos to creator playlists. “You’ve watched consistently for 6 weeks — here’s a structured playlist to go deeper.”

Week 12 — The Pathbreaker

Graduates from creator playlists to broader category playlists. YouTube is not judging expertise — it is simply matching recommendation format to demonstrated behaviour depth.

Key Innovation: Three opt-in notifications only, each firing with the specific recommended video or playlist attached — so the user never opens YouTube from a streak notification and has to decide what to watch next. The pathway is always pre-loaded.

Section 10

Solution 3 — Shorts-to-Long-Form Contextual Bridge

Vertical Solution: directly addresses P1 — passive discovery failure and the format-switching gap

What It Is

A behaviour-triggered bridge card that appears inside the Shorts feed at the moment a user has demonstrated genuine topic interest — after watching a Short to 95% completion and having watched 3 or more Shorts on the same topic in one session. Instead of showing another random Short, the platform surfaces a Deep Dive Preview card: a 45–90 second highlight clip from a related long-form video, displayed in the same vertical Shorts format, with a visible “Watch Full Video” CTA and the full video length shown clearly.

How It Works

  • Trigger: 95% Short completion + 3+ same-topic Shorts in one session. Both signals must be true, preventing irrelevant bridge cards after casual scrolling.
  • Frequency: One bridge card per 5 Shorts maximum to preserve the Shorts session experience.
  • Learning system: If the user exits the long video in under 60 seconds, similar suggestions are reduced. If they watch more than 5 minutes, similar recommendations are boosted.
  • Risk control: If Shorts session length drops by more than 5% in A/B testing, bridge card frequency is reduced before broader rollout.

Key Innovation: This is not a same-creator link. Every existing YouTube mechanism that connects Shorts to long-form is creator-dependent. This bridge is system-level, cross-creator, and behaviour-triggered. It activates on what the user just demonstrated interest in, not on who made the content. The discovery surface is intent-matched, not subscription-matched.

Section 11

Solution 4 — Creator Success Suite

Lateral Solution: directly addresses P2 (padding loop) and P3 (creators quitting long-form)

What It Is

A set of AI-powered tools built directly into YouTube Studio that reduces the effort, cost, and skill barrier of making long-form content — addressing P3 at the supply level — while simultaneously introducing a pre-publish Content Quality Score that penalises padding before a video goes live, addressing P2 at the source.

Two Core Pillars

AI Creation Lab

Built-in toolkit inside YouTube Studio. Includes: AI script generator, AI video editor suggesting cuts and chapter markers, auto-captions in 12 Indian languages plus English, AI thumbnail generator, and SEO-optimised title and description drafts. Tool access is tiered by subscriber count.

Content Quality Score

Runs before every publish — scoring hook strength, pacing, depth, and engagement potential on a 0–100 scale. Gives creators timestamped suggestions like “trim intro from 1:10–2:00 to improve hook.” The score is not public and not linked to monetisation. Purely advisory.

Key Innovation: The Content Quality Score is a pre-publish intervention — the only point in the creator workflow where the platform can influence content quality before it damages viewer trust. Every other platform mechanism responds to underperformance after the fact. This acts before the video goes live, turning quality improvement into a gamified pre-publish ritual rather than a post-hoc analytics exercise.

Success Metrics

Primary Metrics (North Star)

Long-Form Completion Rate Currently ~42% avg. Target: increase by 10+ percentage points within 90 days of Deep Dive Tab launch.
Session Abandonment Rate Currently 19% overall, 29% for 18–24. Target: reduce abandonment by 20% within first quarter.

Solution-Level Metrics

Deep Dive Tab

  • Tab adoption rate (% of MAU who visit at least once)
  • Long-form videos started via Deep Dive vs. Home
  • Search time per session (target: reduce from 12 min)

Viewing Streak

  • Week 3 retention rate of streak participants
  • Week 6 completion rate (milestone unlock)
  • Weekly long-form sessions per streak user vs. non-streak

Shorts-to-Long Bridge

  • Bridge card tap rate (% of shown cards that are tapped)
  • Post-bridge long-form watch time (>5 min threshold)
  • Shorts session length impact (must stay within −5%)

Creator Success Suite

  • Long-form upload frequency per creator (before/after)
  • Average Content Quality Score at publish
  • Creator long-form completion rate vs. control group
Section 13

Trade-offs & Limitations

What This PRD Does Not Solve

  • Ad frequency and unskippable ads: The most cited frustration in open-ended survey responses. But changing ad behaviour is a monetisation decision requiring a separate workstream with different stakeholders. Attempting to fix it here would stall discovery improvements that are ready to ship.
  • TV and desktop experience: Long-form thrives on CTV (73% of TV watch time). But improving that experience only matters if users can first discover content on mobile, where the journey begins.
  • The Shorts–Long-Form transition rate: The single most important unverified metric in this entire PRD. YouTube has never disclosed what % of long-form views originate from a Shorts session. The Shorts-to-Long Bridge solution is built on the hypothesis that this transition is possible and desirable — but that hypothesis must be treated as the #1 assumption to test in A/B testing before broader rollout.

Dependency Risks

  • Deep Dive Tab requires the recommendation engine to support completion-rate-weighted ranking separately from the main feed — a non-trivial algorithmic change.
  • The Shorts-to-Long Bridge requires topic similarity signals to be shared across the decoupled Shorts and long-form algorithm stacks.
  • Creator Success Suite relies on LLM-based content analysis of video scripts, audio, and pacing patterns — model accuracy will vary by genre and language, particularly for non-English Indian content.

The architectural constraint that shaped all four solutions: Users will not leave their existing feed for a new destination. This is proved by Instagram IGTV, Netflix Play Something, and every other bolted-on long-form feature in the competitor graveyard. Every solution in this PRD is native to a surface users already visit — reducing friction inside the journey they are already on.
