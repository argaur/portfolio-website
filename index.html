Product Principles

The manifesto for strategic logic — 7 principles, one capstone

I approach product work the way an architect approaches a building — first understand the forces acting on the system, then design the structure. Features are load-bearing walls, not decoration.

01

Purpose Before Aesthetics

Define what the artifact must prove before any visual choice is made. Form follows function — a rule I learned drawing buildings years before I wrote my first PRD.

02

Systems Thinking First

Features are the visible 10%. The system underneath — data flow, decision rights, escalation paths — is the 90% that decides whether a feature ships value or noise. Design for the ripple, not the splash.

03

Complexity Is Failure

If users need training, the design failed first. Don’t pretend the problem is simple — make complexity navigable. The best interfaces expose the right three of the fourteen edge cases, deliberately.

04

Build For the Constraint

Site, brief, code, budget — five years of architecture taught me constraints don’t limit the design, they are the design. Same rule in software: the constraint is the brief.

05

Build for the 6 PM Manager

Enterprise software is judged at peak load, not in demos. Three years shipping BPO platforms to 5,000+ users taught me: every screen must answer one question someone needs at 6 PM on a Tuesday, under pressure, with no time to think.

06

Ship to Learn

The first version is a question. I don’t just write PRDs — I ship working prototypes and let the data answer. Three are live in production today. Iterate relentlessly.

07

Capstone Principle

The Inevitability Test

If the design language could be transplanted to a different product without feeling wrong, it was imposed, not derived. Every choice must be earned. I learned this critiquing buildings — it holds for every product surface since.
